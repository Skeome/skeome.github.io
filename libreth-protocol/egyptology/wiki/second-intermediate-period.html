The Age of Five Powers (c. 1650–1550 B.C.E.)

The Second Intermediate Period is often simplified as the era of Hyksos rule, but it was in fact a complex period of political fragmentation in which Egypt was divided into at least five concurrent powers. This era followed the collapse of the Middle Kingdom's centralized authority during the 13th Dynasty.

The Hyksos (15th Dynasty)

Rulers of Levantine origin (Heqa-khasut, "Rulers of Foreign Lands") who controlled the Delta from Avaris. They were not mere "invaders" but a migrant population that gradually assumed power. They introduced the composite bow, the horse-drawn chariot, and advanced bronze-working techniques to Egypt.

The Theban 17th Dynasty

Indigenous Egyptian rulers who maintained traditional Kemetic culture in Upper Egypt. They initially paid tribute to the Hyksos but eventually launched a "holy war" of liberation, viewing the Hyksos as agents of Isfet.

The Kerma Kingdom (Kush)

A powerful Nubian state to the south. During this period, Kush reached its maximum extent, occasionally allying with the Hyksos to squeeze the Theban dynasty from both sides.

The Abydos Dynasty

A short-lived, local independent dynasty that controlled the sacred region of Abydos. Their existence confirms the total breakdown of centralized control across the Nile valley.

The War of Liberation & Military Heka

The conflict reached its peak under Seqenenre Tao, whose mummy shows he died from brutal axe and spear wounds—likely executed or killed in battle. His sons, Kamose and Ahmose I, continued the struggle.

The Hyksos Exchange

Despite the conflict, this era saw a massive influx of ideas. The Hyksos period is when many Middle Kingdom texts (like the Rhind Mathematical Papyrus) were recopied and preserved. The Egyptian adoption of Hyksos military technology transformed the pharaoh from a ritual overseer into an imperial warrior-god, setting the stage for the New Kingdom.
