What is Gnosticism?

Gnosticism (from Greek gnōsis, "knowledge") refers to a diverse set of religious and philosophical movements that flourished in Roman Egypt during the 2nd and 3rd centuries C.E. Its central tenet is that salvation is achieved through direct, experiential knowledge of the divine, rather than through faith or adherence to religious laws.

Gnostic thought is characterized by a dualistic worldview, contrasting the perfect spiritual realm (the Pleroma) with the flawed material world, which was believed to have been created by an inferior or malevolent deity known as the Demiurge.

The Kemetic Connection

Gnosticism heavily incorporated Egyptian cosmological elements into its complex systems. Many of the Gnostic "Aeons" and "Archons" have their roots in Egyptian deities and cosmological concepts:

The Demiurge as Isfet

The Gnostic Demiurge (often called Ialdabaoth) can be seen as a distortion of the Egyptian concept of Isfet or a corrupted version of the creator god Ptah/Atum.

The Sophia Myth

The story of Sophia (Wisdom) falling from the Pleroma and her eventual restoration has parallels with the Egyptian myth of the "Distant Goddess" (Hathor/Sekhmet) returning from the desert.

The Nag Hammadi Library

Discovered in 1945 in Upper Egypt, the Nag Hammadi Library consists of thirteen Coptic codices containing over fifty Gnostic treatises, including the Gospel of Thomas, the Secret Book of John, and the Gospel of Mary. These texts confirmed that Gnosticism was a deeply rooted and intellectually sophisticated movement with strong Egyptian linguistic and cultural ties.
