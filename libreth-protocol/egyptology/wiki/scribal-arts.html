The Sacred Profession

To be a Scribe (sesh) in ancient Egypt was to hold a position of immense prestige and magical potential. Scribes were the administrators of the state, but also the keepers of the "Divine Words" (Medu Netjer). The act of writing was a creative act, paralleling the god Ptah's creation of the world.

Tools of the Trade

The Palette (Gesti)

Often made of wood or stone, containing depressions for red and black ink. The palette was considered an offering table to Thoth. To "pour a libation" from the water pot was a ritual act before writing.

Ink and Reed

Black ink (carbon) was used for the main text, representing eternity and stability. Red ink (ochre) was used for titles, headings, and dangerous words (like the name of Apep), representing power and life.

The Satire of the Trades (Instruction of Kheti)

The most famous defense of the scribal profession is the Middle Kingdom text known as the Satire of the Trades. In it, the sage Kheti describes the miseries of every other profession—the blacksmith who "stinks of fish," the weaver who is "more wretched than a woman," the stone-cutter whose "arms are worn out"—to convince his son that "there is no office free of supervisors except that of the scribe."

Immortal through Ink

Egyptian thought proposed a unique form of immortality through writing. A New Kingdom papyrus (Chester Beatty IV) declares: "A man has perished, his corpse is dust... but the scribe causes him to be remembered through the mouth of the reciter. Better is a book than a well-built house or a tomb in the west." This establishes the scribe as the primary agent of the Ren's eternal existence.

Thoth: The Great Stenographer

Thoth was believed to be the "inventor of languages" and the stenographer of the gods. Every scribe was his earthly representative. In the Hall of Judgment, it is Thoth who records the verdict. The scribal profession was therefore the ultimate act of Observation and Record, anchoring the transient events of time into the eternal memory of the cosmos.
