The Theological Earthquake (c. 2181–2055 BCE)

The collapse of centralized Old Kingdom authority was a trauma recorded in texts like the Admonitions of Ipuwer: "The bowman is ready. The wrongdoer is everywhere." This was not just political chaos but a crisis of Ma'at. If the pharaoh was no longer the sole guarantor of order, the individual had to secure their own survival.

Rise of the Nomarchs

Power shifted to local governors (nomarchs). The autobiography of Ankhtifi of Mo'alla boasts of his ability to feed his people while others starved, replacing the king as the provider. This shift in biography—from "I served the king" to "I saved my city"—marked a new era of individualism.

Political Realism: The Teaching for Merikare

The Teaching for King Merikare is a seminal text from this period, written by a Herakleopolitan king to his son. It is a work of startling political realism, advising on how to suppress rebellion, secure borders, and deal with the "wretched Asiatic." It marks a shift from the divine confidence of the Old Kingdom to a pragmatic, hard-edged statecraft.

Democratization of the Afterlife

Funerary privileges previously reserved for royalty were adopted by local elites. The Coffin Texts appeared, adapting the royal Pyramid Texts for private use. This democratization meant that any deceased person who could afford the rituals could become an Osiris and achieve Akh status, provided they passed the tribunal of the gods.
