The Healing Arts of the Per Ankh

Egyptian medicine was inseparable from Heka. Prescriptions often combined empirical observation and herbal remedies with magical incantations to neutralize the spiritual causes of illness. These documents were primarily managed within the House of Life.

EBERS PAPYRUS (c. 1550 BCE)

A massive collection of over 700 prescriptions. It includes internal medicine, ophthalmology, and dentistry. [Digital Facsimile: Leipzig]

EDWIN SMITH PAPYRUS (c. 1600 BCE)

A surgical text featuring 48 case studies. Uniquely empirical, it uses a systematic prognosis system: "An ailment I will treat" (favorable), "An ailment with which I will contend" (uncertain), or "An ailment not to be treated" (untreatable). [Digital Facsimile: NYAM]

LONDON MEDICAL PAPYRUS (c. 1300 BCE)

A critical document for the "unified theory" of Kemetic medicine, containing 25 material prescriptions and 61 magical spells in a single scroll—proving that physician and magician were one.

HEARST PAPYRUS (c. 1450 BCE)

Housed at UC Berkeley, this papyrus focuses on the treatment of blood, hair, and skin conditions, as well as fractures and bites.

KAHUN PAPYRUS (c. 1800 BCE)

The world's oldest gynecological and veterinary text, focusing on fertility, pregnancy, and obstetric complications.

Physicians of Sekhmet

Doctors were often members of the priesthood, particularly associated with the goddess Sekhmet (goddess of plague and healing). They were trained to act as "Kher-Heb" (Lector Priests), reading sacred scrolls to stabilize the patient's field.
