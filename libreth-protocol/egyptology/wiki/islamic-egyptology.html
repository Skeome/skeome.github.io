The Arabic Interest in Kemet

Medieval Arabic scholars were fascinated by ancient Kemet, viewing its monuments and Hieroglyphs as repositories of coded alchemical and philosophical wisdom rather than mere phonetic scripts.

Key Figures

  • Dhu al-Nun al-Misri (796–859 CE): An Egyptian mystic who claimed to read hieroglyphs through allegorical interpretation. He is a founding figure of Sufism and is credited with bringing alchemical knowledge into Islamic thought.
  • Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber): Transmitted and transformed the Greco-Egyptian Alchemical tradition, developing elaborate numerical and astrological systems that influenced both Islamic and European science.
  • Ibn Wahshiyah (c. 904 CE): Author of Shauq al-Mustaham, an early attempt to decipher hieroglyphs. While primarily symbolic, his work represents a serious effort to unlock the "Words of God."

The Treasure Trove of Knowledge

Arabic scholarship maintained the survival of Hermetic thought through the Middle Ages. They saw the Nile Valley as the cradle of science, particularly chemistry (al-kimiya), astronomy, and medicine, preserving traditions that would later trigger the European Renaissance.
