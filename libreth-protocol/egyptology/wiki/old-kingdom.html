The Golden Age of Solar Theology (c. 2686–2181 BCE)

The Old Kingdom represents the first great crystallization of Kemetic theology into monumental form. Often called the "Age of Pyramids," it was a period where the state operated as a theological machine, with the pharaoh maintaining Ma'at to ensure the sun rose and the Nile flooded.

Dynastic Progression

3rd Dynasty: The Stone Revolution

King Djoser and his architect Imhotep built the Step Pyramid at Saqqara, turning the royal tomb from a mud-brick mastaba into a stone stairway to the stars. This marked the beginning of monumental stone architecture.

4th Dynasty: The Giza Giants

Sneferu perfected the true pyramid form (Meidum, Bent, Red). His successors—Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure—built the Giza plateau. This era saw the height of centralized royal power, with the king viewed as a living god.

5th Dynasty: Sons of Re

A theological shift occurred. Kings began building Sun Temples at Abusir dedicated directly to Re. The pyramid size decreased, but the interior decoration—the Pyramid Texts—appeared under Unas.

6th Dynasty: The Long Decline

The incredibly long reign of Pepi II (90+ years) saw the erosion of central authority. Governors (Nomarchs) became hereditary princes, leading to the fragmentation of the First Intermediate Period.

The Pyramid Texts & Cannibal Hymn

Appearing in the 5th Dynasty, the Pyramid Texts are the oldest religious corpus in history. They are not merely prayers but "transfiguration spells" designed to blast the king's soul into the celestial realm.

"Unas is he who eats men and lives on the gods... Unas eats their magic and swallows their spirits. Their great ones are for his morning meal, their middle-sized ones for his evening meal..."

— The Cannibal Hymn (Utterance 273-274)

This fierce text demonstrates the raw, undiluted nature of Old Kingdom Heka: the king literally consumes the divine essence to ensure his own immortality.

Administration: The Vizier

The state was run by the Tjati (Vizier), the supreme judge and architect. Figures like Imhotep, Ptahhotep (author of the Instruction of Ptahhotep), and Kagemni set the standard for the "Silent Man"—the ethical bureaucrat who upheld Ma'at through silence and efficiency.
