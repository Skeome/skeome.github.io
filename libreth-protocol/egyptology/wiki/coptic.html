I. The Ontology of Coptic — A Linguistic Survival

Coptic (ⲙⲉⲧⲣⲉⲙⲛ̀ⲭⲏⲙⲓ - metremenkhemi, "the language of the people of Egypt") represents the final evolutionary stage of the Ancient Egyptian language. Developed during the 2nd century CE, it served as the primary spoken and written language of Egypt until the 10th century, when it was gradually superseded by Arabic.

In the context of the Libreth Protocol, Coptic is viewed as a linguistic anchor. It is the only stage of the Egyptian language that has never truly died, persisting to this day as the liturgical language of the Coptic Orthodox Church. It preserves the grammar, syntax, and much of the vocabulary of the Pharaohs, reframed for a new era.

II. The Script — Greek Letters, Egyptian Soul

// LINGUISTIC SHIFT //

The shift to the Coptic script marked the first time the Egyptian language was recorded with full vocalic notation, including vowels, which were omitted in Hieroglyphs and Demotic.

The Coptic script is composed of the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet, supplemented by 7 additional characters derived from Demotic to represent sounds unique to the Egyptian language (such as sh, f, kh, h, j, tj, and ti).

This transition was not merely practical; it was a systematic re-encoding. By adopting an alphabetic system, the Egyptians were able to transcribe the precise vibrations of their spoken language with a clarity that the consonantal scripts could not achieve.

III. The Key to Decipherment — Champollion's Breakthrough

Knowledge of Coptic was the essential tool that allowed Jean-François Champollion to decipher the Rosetta Stone in 1822. Having mastered Coptic from a young age, Champollion realized that the language of the liturgy was the same language as the hieroglyphs.

He famously observed: "Coptic is only Egyptian written in Greek characters." Without the survival of Coptic within the Church, the phonetic values of the hieroglyphs might have remained a mystery for centuries longer.

IV. Dialects and the Alchemical Tradition

Coptic is divided into several major dialects, the most significant of which are Sahidic (Upper Egypt), Bohairic (Lower Egypt), and Akhmimic.

AKHMIMIC & ALCHEMY

The Akhmimic dialect, centered in Panopolis (modern Akhmim), was the language of the earliest alchemical treatises. Figures like Zosimos of Panopolis worked in this linguistic milieu, bridging traditional temple secrets with the emerging Gnostic and Hermetic terminologies.

BOHAIRIC LITURGY

Bohairic eventually became the standard liturgical dialect of the Coptic Church. It is through Bohairic that the ancient ritual chants and prayers — many of which maintain the rhythmic structure of Pharaonic hymns — have been preserved.

V. The Bridge to Ancient Pronunciation

Because Coptic includes vowels, it serves as the primary source for the reconstruction of Ancient Egyptian phonology. By analyzing the Coptic descendants of ancient words, linguists can approximate how the language was spoken in earlier periods.
