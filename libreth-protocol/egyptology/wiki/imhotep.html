The Historical Figure

Imhotep (fl. 2650 BCE) was the vizier and chief architect of Pharaoh Djoser (3rd Dynasty). He is credited with inventing stone architecture by designing the Step Pyramid complex at Saqqara, the first monumental stone structure in history.

The Many Titles of Imhotep

Imhotep held a unprecedented range of offices: Chancellor of the King of Lower Egypt, First after the King of Upper Egypt, Administrator of the Great Palace, Hereditary Noble, High Priest of Heliopolis, Chief of Sculptors, and Chief of Seers. This polymathic status made him the historical prototype for the Hermetic Sage.

The Medical Legend

While no contemporary medical writings survive from his hand, later tradition attributed the founding of Egyptian medicine to Imhotep. He was believed to have authored the original texts that became the Edwin Smith Papyrus. His deification as a god of healing (son of Ptah) was a recognition of his mastery over both the structural (architecture) and biological (medicine) laws of nature.

Apotheosis: Becoming a God

Unlike most non-royals, Imhotep was deified two thousand years after his death. By the Late Period, he was worshipped as the son of Ptah and a god of medicine and healing.

Imhotep-Asclepius

The Greeks identified Imhotep with their own god of medicine, Asclepius. His cult centers, particularly at Memphis and Philae, became major pilgrimage sites for incubation healing rituals, where the sick would sleep in the temple to receive a cure from the god.
