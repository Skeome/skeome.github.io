The Imperial Era (c. 1550–1075 BCE)

The New Kingdom (Dynasties 18-20) represents the absolute peak of Egyptian power, wealth, and theological complexity. Having expelled the Hyksos, the pharaohs transformed Egypt into an international empire, extending control from the Euphrates in the north to the Fourth Cataract of the Nile in the south.

Hatshepsut & Thutmose III

The 18th Dynasty saw the rise of the female pharaoh Hatshepsut, who emphasized trade (the Punt expedition) and built her stunning mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari. Her successor, Thutmose III (the "Napoleon of Egypt"), conducted 17 military campaigns, cementing Egypt as the world's superpower.

Ramesses II (The Great)

The 19th Dynasty was dominated by Ramesses II, whose 66-year reign saw the construction of Abu Simbel and the Ramesseum. He signed the world's first recorded peace treaty with the Hittites after the Battle of Kadesh.

The Triumph of Amun-Ra

The state god Amun of Thebes merged with the solar deity Ra, creating a supreme "King of the Gods." This synthesis represented the invisible spirit (Amun) manifesting through the visible sun (Ra). The High Priests of Amun at Karnak eventually became so wealthy and powerful that they rivaled the pharaohs themselves, eventually leading to the fragmentation of the Third Intermediate Period.

The Funerary Transition

Pyramids were abandoned in favor of hidden rock-cut tombs in the Valley of the Kings. This shift was motivated by security and a theological desire to place the royal body deep within the earth, mirroring the sun god's journey through the Duat.

The Book of the Dead became the essential roadmap for this journey, emphasizing the Weighing of the Heart as the ultimate test of integrity.
