Conservators of Tradition

The rule of the Nubian pharaohs from Kush (25th Dynasty, c. 747–656 BCE) represents a deliberate act of cultural archaism. Far from "foreign conquerors," the Kushite kings presented themselves as the true preservers of authentic Kemetic tradition, often behaving more orthodox than the Libyan dynasties that preceded them.

The Return to the Old Kingdom

The Kushite kings revived Old Kingdom funerary traditions, including pyramid building (notably at Meroe and Nuri). They promoted a religious conservatism that reached back past the New Kingdom to the "pure" forms of the Old and Middle Kingdoms.

The Memphite Theology was recorded during this era on the Shabaka Stone, which the king claimed was a copy of a "worm-eaten" ancient papyrus.

Archaeological Legacy

Their reign saw massive restoration projects at Karnak and other major cult centers. The Kushite pharaohs saw themselves as the restorers of Ma'at in a fractured land, utilizing their power to re-center the Nile Valley on its primordial origins.
