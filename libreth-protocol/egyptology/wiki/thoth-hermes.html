Thoth [Djehuty]

The Egyptian deity Thoth (Djehuty) was the lord of writing, wisdom, magic, the moon, and knowledge. Often depicted ibis-headed or as a baboon, he was the scribe of the gods and the inventor of hieroglyphs (Medu Netjer).

In the afterlife, Thoth played a critical role as the recorder of the Weighing of the Heart, noting the verdict of the scales with divine impartiality.

The Evolution to Trismegistus

The epithet "Trismegistus" (thrice-greatest) is rooted in an Egyptian linguistic convention found as early as 172 BCE, where Thoth was described as "the greatest and greatest god, great Hermes". This title reflected his status as the premier philosopher, priest, and king.

Through the process of syncretism in Alexandria, Thoth was merged with the Greek Hermes. Both deities shared attributes as inventors of writing, patrons of intellectual activity, and psychopomps who guided souls between realms.

Patron of the House of Life

Thoth was the patron deity of the House of Life (Per Ankh), where the sacred scrolls and magical documents were managed. He was believed to have revealed all knowledge of astronomy, geometry, architecture, and medicine to humanity.
