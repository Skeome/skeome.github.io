What is a Shabti?

A shabti (also shawabti or ushabti) is a small anthropoid figurine found in many Egyptian tombs. Their purpose was to perform manual labor on behalf of the deceased in the afterlife, particularly agricultural work in the Field of Reeds (Aaru).

The name ushabti (Ancient Egyptian wšbtj) means "the one who answers," referring to the figurine's role in responding to the deceased's call for help.

Evolution of the Divine Set

The use of shabtis grew from solitary figurines in the Middle Kingdom to standardized sets in the Late Period. A complete elite set eventually consisted of 401 figurines: one worker for each of the 365 days of the year, plus 36 "overseer" (rais) figures to manage each ten-day work group.

Material Hierarchy

The material composition of a shabti reflected the status of the deceased. While faience (blue-glazed ceramic) was the most common, royal and elite shabtis were crafted from limestone, alabaster, wood, or even cast bronze, each believed to possess distinct alchemical resonances in the afterlife.

Spell 6: The Activation

The shabti figurine was brought to life by the recitation of Spell 6 of the Book of the Dead, which was often inscribed directly onto the body of the figure:

"O shabti, if the Osiris [Name] is called upon to do any work in the god’s domain—to cultivate the fields, to water the banks, to transport sand from east to west—‘Here am I’ you shall say, whenever you are called."

This spell magically transferred the obligation of agricultural labor in the Field of Reeds (Sekhet-Aaru) from the soul of the deceased to the figurine.
