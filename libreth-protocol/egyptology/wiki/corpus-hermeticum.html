The Wisdom of Alexandria

The Corpus Hermeticum is a collection of 17 Greek treatises, primarily dialogues between Hermes Trismegistus and his disciples (Tat, Asclepius) or the divine Nous (Mind). They represent the philosophical core of the Hermetic tradition.

The Tractate Index

CH I: POIMANDRES (The Vision)

Hermes receives a revelation from the "Shepherd of Men" (Nous). It details the Ascent of the Soul: as the spirit rises through the seven planetary spheres (Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn), it sheds its negative traits (greed, deceit, pride) until it reaches the Eighth Sphere—the fixed stars—to unite with the One.

CH XIII: PALINGENESIA (The Second Birth)

Hermes instructs his son Tat on the process of rebirth. This is not a biological event but a psychological and spiritual dismantling of the material self. By invoking the "ten powers of God" (Knowledge, Joy, Self-Control, etc.), the aspirant displaces the "twelve tormentors" (Ignorance, Grief, Injustice, etc.), achieving a transfigured state of consciousness comparable to the Kemetic Akh.

THE ASCLEPIUS (The Perfect Word)

A Latin fragment of a lost Greek original. It contains the famous "Lament of Hermes"—a prophecy describing the future decline of Egyptian religion—and the practice of drawing down divine souls into statues to animate them.

Authenticity and Origins

While once believed to be from pre-dynastic Egypt, philological analysis by Isaac Casaubon in 1614 proved they were written in the early centuries CE. However, modern scholarship confirms they authentically reflect the initiatory traditions of the Egyptian priesthood (House of Life) adapted for a Hellenistic audience.
