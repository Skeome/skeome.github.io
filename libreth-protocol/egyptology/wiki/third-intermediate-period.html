Overview (1075–656 B.C.E.)

The Third Intermediate Period followed the death of Ramesses XI and the collapse of the New Kingdom. It is characterized by a lack of a single centralized authority, with power divided between the pharaohs ruling from Tanis in the north and the High Priests of Amun at Thebes in the south. This era saw the rise of Libyan (Meshwesh) and later Nubian (Kushite) dynasties.

Despite the political fragmentation, this period was a time of significant cultural and religious development. The "Theban Theocracy" represented a unique moment where the god Amun was considered the true king of Egypt, ruling through oracles.

Key Dynasties

The Libyan Dynasties (22nd–24th)

Descendants of Libyan mercenaries who had settled in the Delta. Shoshenq I (Biblical Shishak) famously campaigned in the Levant and sacked Jerusalem.

The Kushite Dynasty (25th)

Rulers from Napata (modern Sudan) who conquered Egypt to restore Ma'at. Figures like Piy and Taharqa oversaw a major cultural renaissance and temple construction.

The Theban Theocracy

In the south, the High Priests of Amun at Thebes became de facto rulers, often linked to the Tanite pharaohs by marriage. This period saw the "cachette" of royal mummies—the systematic re-wrapping and hiding of New Kingdom pharaohs (like Ramesses II and Thutmose III) in secret locations like the DB320 tomb to protect them from widespread tomb robbery.
