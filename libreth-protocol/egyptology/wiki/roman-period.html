Overview

The Roman Period began with the defeat of Cleopatra VII and Mark Antony by Octavian (Augustus) at the Battle of Actium. Egypt became the personal province of the Roman Emperor, serving as the "breadbasket of Rome." This era witnessed the final crystallization of Egyptian religious thought, the birth of Hermeticism, and the eventual rise of Christianity.

During this time, the Roman Emperors were depicted in traditional Egyptian style as pharaohs on temple walls (e.g., at Dendera, Philae, and Kom Ombo). These temples reached their architectural peak, with massive astronomical ceilings and intricate relief carvings.

Key Developments

The Rise of Serapis and Isis

The cult of Isis spread throughout the Roman Empire, while Serapis, a deity created during the Ptolemaic period as a bridge between Greek and Egyptian religion, remained a central figure.

Synthesis of Wisdom

Alexandria remained a vibrant intellectual hub where Neoplatonism, Gnosticism, and early Christian theology intersected with ancient Kemetic metaphysics.

The Last Hieroglyphs

The final known hieroglyphic inscription was carved on the walls of the Temple of Philae on August 24, 394 C.E.. The decree of Theodosius I in 391 C.E., which ordered the closure of pagan temples, effectively signaled the end of the traditional Egyptian priesthood and the functional use of the sacred script.

However, the underlying metaphysical concepts did not vanish; they transitioned into the Coptic tradition and the burgeoning Hermetic literature, which would later fuel the European Renaissance.
