Anthropology of the Spirit

Ancient Egyptian thought understood the human being not as a simple duality of body and soul, but as a sophisticated composite of several distinct spiritual bodies and essences. Each part played a specific role in life and the journey through the afterlife.

KHAT [ḫt]

The Physical Form. The corporeal body, subject to decay but preserved through mummification to serve as an anchor for the soul's components.

KA [kȝ]

The Vital Double. The life force born with a person. It requires food and drink offerings after death for continued sustenance. Associated with the "astral body."

BA [bȝ]

The Personality Soul. Often depicted as a human-headed hawk. It represents the individual's character and has the ability to travel between the living and dead worlds.

AKH [ꜣḫ]

The Radiant Body. The transfigured, immortal spirit created when the Ka and Ba are successfully united through proper ritual. It dwells among the stars.

REN [rn]

The True Name. The essence and identity of the being. Erasing a name from a monument was considered a "second death," total annihilation of the soul.

AB [ib]

The Heart. The seat of intelligence, conscience, and emotion. It is the only organ left inside the body to be weighed against the feather of Ma'at.

KHAIBIT [šwt]

The Shadow Body. An integral part of the identity capable of independent movement, representing the subconscious or hidden aspects.

SEKHEM [sḫm]

Personal Power. The ethereal personification of life force, representing the ability to overcome obstacles and exert will.
