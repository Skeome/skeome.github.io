Anthropology of the Spirit

Ancient Egyptian thought understood the human being not as a simple duality of body and soul, but as a sophisticated composite of several distinct spiritual bodies and essences. Each part played a specific role in life and the journey through the afterlife.

KHAT [ḫt]

The Physical Form. The corporeal body, subject to decay but preserved through mummification to serve as an anchor for the soul's components.

KA [kȝ]

The Vital Double. The life force born with a person. Khnum fashions the Ka on his potter's wheel simultaneously with the physical body, making it a literal divine twin. It requires food and drink offerings after death for continued sustenance.

BA [bȝ]

The Personality Soul. Often depicted as a human-headed hawk. It represents the individual's character and has the ability to travel between the living and dead worlds. It must return to the body each night.

AKH [ꜣḫ]

The Radiant Body. The transfigured, immortal spirit created when the Ka and Ba are successfully united through proper ritual. It dwells among the stars as a being of light.

REN [rn]

The True Name. The essence and identity of the being. Holding the Ren provides power over the entity. Erasing a name was considered total annihilation.

AB [ib]

The Heart. The seat of intelligence and conscience. Protected by the Heart Scarab inscribed with Spell 30B, which pleads: "O my heart... rise not up against me as a witness."

KHAIBIT [šwt]

The Shadow Body. A powerful, independent entity. Stepping on a shadow was a magical assault. It could travel at will but required protection from "shadow-eating" demons in the Duat.

SEKHEM [sḫm]

Personal Power. More than just will, it is the mastery to overcome supernatural resistance. It is the force that makes ritual effective even against hostile spiritual entities.
