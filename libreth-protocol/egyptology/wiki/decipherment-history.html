The Speculative Era

Before the 19th-century breakthrough, hieroglyphs were viewed through the lens of Renaissance Neo-Hermeticism. The primary figure of this era was the Jesuit polymath Athanasius Kircher (1602–1680).

Kircher’s "Oedipus Aegyptiacus"

Kircher believed that hieroglyphs were purely allegorical vessels for universal truths. He published translations that were linguistically nonsensical—for example, interpreting a simple name as a complex philosophical statement about the sun and the soul. However, his work was the first to recognize the Continuity of Coptic, correctly asserting that the language of the Christian Egyptians was the final survival of the ancient tongue.

The Rosetta Stone breakthrough

The ability to read Hieroglyphs was lost by the 4th century CE, leading to centuries of the Speculative Era. Scholars like Athanasius Kircher (17th century) believed hieroglyphs were purely allegorical or mystical symbols representing complex theological ideas without phonetic value. While wrong about the linguistics, Kircher correctly realized the continuity between Coptic and ancient Egyptian.

The 1799 discovery of the Rosetta Stone by Napoleon's soldiers provided the essential "trilingual" key: Hieroglyphic, Demotic, and Ancient Greek.

The Rivalry & The Key

Thomas Young: The Polymath

Young was the first to realize that the cartouches contained phonetic spellings of foreign royal names (like Ptolemy). He identified several alphabetic signs but remained convinced that the script was primarily ideographic for indigenous Egyptian words.

Jean-François Champollion: "Je tiens l'affaire!"

Champollion's advantage was his obsession with Coptic from childhood. On September 14, 1822, after comparing the cartouches of Ramses and Thutmose from Abu Simbel, he realized that hieroglyphs were simultaneously phonetic, symbolic, and figurative. Legend says he ran into his brother's office, shouted "Je tiens l'affaire!" ("I've got it!"), and fainted from the mental strain.

The Role of Coptic

Champollion's breakthrough was his realization that the Coptic language was the final stage of the ancient Egyptian tongue. This connection allowed him to bridge the gap between the unknown glyphs and a living voice, enabling the translation of thousands of years of recorded history.
