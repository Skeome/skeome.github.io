What are the PGM?

The Greek Magical Papyri (Latin: Papyri Graecae Magicae, or PGM) is a collection of magical scrolls discovered in Egypt, dating from the 2nd century B.C.E. to the 5th century C.E. They provide an unprecedented look at the magical practice of late antiquity, representing a profound synthesis of Egyptian, Greek, Jewish, and early Christian traditions.

While primarily written in Greek, many of the texts include passages in Demotic (often called the Papyri Magicae Demoticae, or PDM) and contain numerous references to Egyptian deities and mythological concepts, often in highly syncretized forms.

The Magical Techniques: Voces Magicae

The PGM utilized a highly sophisticated system of Voces Magicae—"magical words" or "barbarous names of power." These were strings of syllables (often including Hebrew, Egyptian, and unknown phonemes) that were believed to vibrate at the exact frequency of specific gods or demons. By vocalizing these names with Hu (Authoritative Utterance), the magician could compel supernatural forces to manifest.

The Mithras Liturgy (PGM IV.475-829)

One of the most famous tractates within the PGM, it describes a method for the Ascent of the Soul. The practitioner uses rhythmic breathing and seven "vowel chants" to rise through the planetary spheres, eventually achieving a vision of the solar god. This text bridges the gap between traditional Egyptian star-ascent and Hellenistic mystery religions.

Significance as a Source Bridge

The PGM are a major hub for cross-linking ancient magical systems. They show how Egyptian Heka was adapted into the Hellenistic framework of Theurgy, and how traditional Egyptian deities like Thoth, Isis, and Osiris were integrated into a broader, Mediterranean magical language.

These papyri were likely part of the libraries of professional magician-priests who continued to practice in secret or as private consultants long after the official temple cults had declined.
