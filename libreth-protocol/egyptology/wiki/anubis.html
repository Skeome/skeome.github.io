I. The Jackal God — Pre-Osirian Supremacy

Anubis (Ancient Egyptian: Inpu) is one of the oldest and most consistently venerated deities in the Kemetic pantheon. Depicted as a jackal or a jackal-headed man, he was originally the supreme Lord of the Dead during the Early Dynastic period and the Old Kingdom. His title, Khenty-Imentiu ("Foremost of the Westerners"), marked him as the presiding authority over the necropolis and the transition of the soul.

His signature black color was not a literal representation of jackal fur (which is reddish-gold) but a theological statement. Black symbolized the fertile silt of the Nile and the transformative potential of decay — the promise that life would emerge from the darkness of the tomb. Anubis is thus the god of transmutation, overseeing the threshold between the physical and the spiritual.

II. The Inventor of Embalming — The Weryt

// TEXTUAL CONTEXT //

The Osiris myth attributes the first mummification to Anubis, who reassembled the body of the murdered king, establishing the prototype for all subsequent funerary rites.

Anubis presided over the Weryt ("The Place of Pure Embalming"). In Kemetic thought, mummification was not merely a means of body preservation; it was a high-level alchemical operation designed to transform the rotting Khat (physical body) into an eternal, transfigured vessel capable of anchoring the Ka and Ba.

THE 70-DAY CYCLE

The embalming process lasted exactly 70 days, corresponding to the period during which the star Sirius (Sothis) was absent from the night sky before its heliacal rising. This synchronized the work of the embalmer with the cosmic rhythm of death and rebirth.

THE IMIUT FETISH

A sacred object consisting of a stuffed animal skin attached to a pole, held in a vessel. It was a potent symbol of Anubis's power to regenerate life from the remnants of death, present in every royal tomb.

III. Psychopomp and the Hall of Judgment

As the Guardian of the Scales, Anubis plays a critical role in the Weighing of the Heart ceremony. He is the one who leads the deceased into the Hall of Two Truths, checks the balance of the scales against the feather of Ma'at, and ensures that the judgment is performed with absolute precision.

Unlike many other psychopomps who merely guide, Anubis is an active arbiter. He protects the deceased from the 'Second Death' and guards the entrance to the Duat. He is the master of the Threshold, the one who knows the passwords to every gate and the secret names of the guardians.

VI. Anubis in the Greek Magical Papyri (PGM)

The veneration of Anubis persisted well into the Roman period, where he was often syncretized with Hermes to form Hermanubis. In the Greek Magical Papyri, Anubis is a primary paredros (magical assistant). He is invoked by magicians to:

  • Fetch the Ba of a spirit for communication.
  • Reveal hidden treasures or secrets.
  • Bind malevolent forces through his authority over the dead.
  • Provide protection during theurgical operations.

VII. Alchemical Associations

In the context of the Alchemy entry, Anubis is the personification of the Nigredo (the Black Work). He represents the stage of putrefaction and dissolution that must precede purification and rebirth. His mastery over the preservation of the body mirrors the alchemical goal of creating 'incorruptible matter.'
