I. The Jackal God — Pre-Osirian Supremacy and the Colour of Transformation

Anubis — Ancient Egyptian Inpu or Anpu (𓇋𓈖𓄿𓊪𓅱𓃣) — is among the oldest continuously venerated deities in the Kemetic pantheon, his image appearing in the earliest known funerary contexts of the Predynastic and Early Dynastic periods (c. 3100 BCE and before). Long before Osiris rose to prominence as the supreme Lord of the Dead — a process substantially complete by the Middle Kingdom — Anubis held that title alone. His Old Kingdom epithet Khenty-Imentiu ("Foremost of the Westerners") identified him as the presiding authority over all who had journeyed to the West — the direction of the setting sun and of death — and over the necropoleis that lined the desert edges of the Nile valley.

The jackal was not an arbitrary choice of form. Jackals were conspicuous inhabitants of the boundary between the cultivated Black Land (Kemet) and the Red Land (Deshret) of the desert — scavengers who haunted the margins of burial grounds, digging at the edges of shallow graves. Rather than responding to this association with aversion, the Egyptians with characteristic theological sophistication recruited the jackal's power into protective service: Anubis, depicted as the jackal or jackal-headed man who watched over the dead, conscripted the very animal that would otherwise desecrate the grave into its fiercest protector. The creature that scavenged became the guardian that prevented scavenging.

His canonical black colouration requires specific attention. The jackal's actual fur ranges from reddish-gold to grey-brown — never black. The choice of black was deliberate and theologically loaded. In the Kemetic colour-essence system (iwn), black held a dual significance simultaneously opposed to Western interpretations: it was the colour of the fertile Nile silt, the kemet itself, the rich dark earth that yielded all life. It was also the colour of the decomposing body — the Nigredo state — from which the resurrection would emerge. Black in the Egyptian system was not death as termination but death as the fertile void from which new life always emerges. To depict Anubis in black was to identify him with the deepest transformative potential of the death-state: the darkness that is full of generative power, the compost from which the next cycle grows.

// THE ANCIENT SUPREMACY — WHAT ANUBIS WAS BEFORE OSIRIS //

The shift of the supreme funerary role from Anubis to Osiris — which occurred gradually from the Old Kingdom through the Middle Kingdom as the Osirian theological complex expanded — is one of the most significant transformations in Egyptian religious history. In the earliest Pyramid Texts (c. 2400 BCE), Anubis is the primary funerary deity; Osiris appears but does not yet dominate. By the time of the Coffin Texts (c. 2000 BCE), the roles have begun to reverse. By the New Kingdom Book of the Dead, Osiris presides over the Hall of Judgment and Anubis has been repositioned as the master technician within the broader Osirian system.

This repositioning was not a demotion but a functional refinement. Anubis retained and deepened his specific domain — the precise management of the threshold between death and afterlife — while Osiris absorbed the monarchical, judicial, and agricultural dimensions of the lord-of-the-dead role. The result was a more sophisticated system: Anubis as the expert practitioner of transition technology, Osiris as the ultimate judge and sovereign. Their functions are complementary, not competitive, in the mature theology.

II. Divine Parentage — The Contested Origin

The question of Anubis's parentage was genuinely contested in the Egyptian textual tradition, with different sources proposing different genealogies — a reflection of his extreme antiquity and the integration of originally distinct regional traditions into a unified theological system.

OSIRIS AND NEPHTHYS — THE CANONICAL VERSION

The most widely attested genealogy in the developed Osirian mythology makes Anubis the son of Osiris and Nephthys — conceived when Nephthys (Set's wife) disguised herself as Isis and lay with Osiris. This parentage places Anubis in the second generation of the Ennead and explains his presence at Osiris's mummification: he assists his father-figure's transition while also being, cosmologically, the product of a transgression that prefigures the broader disruption Set introduces. Isis, discovering the infant, adopted and raised him — reinforcing the Osirian family network while adding an adoptive-maternal dimension to Anubis's connection to Isis's powerful magical legacy.

RE AS FATHER — THE OLDER TRADITION

Earlier Pyramid Text sources assign Anubis a solar rather than an Osirian parentage — identifying him as the son of Re himself, placing him among the first generation of divine offspring and giving him a more cosmologically senior status. This tradition reflects his pre-Osirian supremacy; a deity who presided over all the dead before Osiris existed logically required a genealogy that predated the Osirian family structure. The tension between these two genealogies was never fully resolved — later theology generally adopted the Nephthys-born version for narrative coherence while implicitly acknowledging the older solar connection through Anubis's continued association with the cyclic solar journey through the Duat.

HESAT AS MOTHER

Some sources identify Hesat — a cow goddess associated with nourishment and the milk of divine sustenance — as Anubis's mother, with Re as father. Hesat is a relatively obscure deity whose primary mythological role was as the divine cow who suckled sacred animals and humans with her life-giving milk. The mother-as-nurturer dimension this adds to Anubis is interesting theologically: it positions the god of death as himself having been tenderly nourished into existence, conferring on him a quality of genuine care for the souls he manages through their vulnerable transition.

THEOLOGICAL IMPLICATION OF THE AMBIGUITY

The contested parentage is not a confusion or an error — it is a theological statement in the form of preserved plurality. Egyptian religion was not a monolithic creed but a living tradition that absorbed regional variants. Anubis's multiple genealogies reflect his genuine antiquity (predating any fixed mythological system), his functional importance across different theological frameworks, and the characteristic Egyptian willingness to hold multiple truths simultaneously without demanding a single authoritative resolution. He is both older than Osiris and of Osiris's family. Both are true at different levels of the myth.

III. The Inventor of Embalming — Sacred Alchemy of the Weryt

// THE WERYT //

Weryt — "The Place of Pure Embalming" — was the designated sacred space where the embalming operation was performed. It was structurally separate from the tomb itself, often a tent or temporary pavilion of pure white linen. The chief embalmer wore an Anubis mask during the most sacred phases of the operation, literally becoming the god.

In the Osiris myth, the first act of mummification was performed by Anubis — working alongside Isis, Nephthys, and Thoth — when he reassembled the scattered body of the slain king, wrapped it in linen, and prepared it for its passage into the Duat. This mythological prototype established the template for all subsequent funerary practice. Every Egyptian embalming was a re-enactment of Anubis's original operation on Osiris; every embalmed corpse was, ritually, Osiris's body; every embalmer was, in the moments of most sacred operation, Anubis himself.

The Kemetic understanding of mummification was not primarily about body preservation as an end in itself. It was an operative alchemical transformation of the highest order — the conversion of the decaying Khat (physical body) into an eternal, transfigured vessel, the Sahu (glorified body), capable of serving as an anchor point for the returning Ka and Ba across the cycles of the afterlife. The physical chemistry of decay was the raw material; Anubis's technical mastery was the catalyst; the Sahu was the gold.

The 70-Day Process — Astronomical and Alchemical Dimensions

The embalming process lasted exactly 70 days — a number that was not arbitrary but astronomically precise. 70 days is the period during which the star Sirius (Sopdet, identified with Isis) disappears below the Egyptian horizon before its annual heliacal rising, which marked the beginning of the Nile flood and the Egyptian New Year. The 70-day embalming thus synchronised the preparation of the body with the cosmic rhythm of Sirius's own death and rebirth — the deceased's transformation occurring in parallel with the star of Isis's annual passage through the Duat.

NATRON — THE PURIFYING SALT

The body was packed in dry natron — a naturally occurring sodium carbonate compound from Wadi Natrun — for approximately 40 days. Natron achieved desiccation (the removal of moisture that bacteria require for decomposition) while simultaneously performing a ritual function: natron was the supreme purification substance of the Egyptian system, used in priestly purification baths, in temple cleansing, and in the preparation of ritual implements. The body was being simultaneously preserved and purified — rendered chemically stable and ritually clean for its transformation.

ORGAN REMOVAL AND THE CANOPIC SYSTEM

The visceral organs — liver, lungs, stomach, intestines — were removed through an incision in the left flank (the embalmer's flint knife was ritually thrown into the Nile afterward, as cutting a sacred body was a technically transgressive act requiring purification). The organs were treated separately, wrapped, and placed in four canopic jars presided over by the Four Sons of Horus (Imsety, Hapy, Duamutef, Qebehsenuef). The heart — the seat of the Ib and the organ of judgment — was the single exception: it was replaced within the body, as it would be needed for the Weighing.

THE BRAIN — DISCARDED INTELLIGENCE

The brain was removed via the nostrils using a hooked instrument and discarded — the Egyptians located thought, emotion, and moral discernment in the heart (Ib), not the head. The brain was considered functionally irrelevant to the afterlife, an embarrassing fact for modern interpreters but entirely consistent with the Kemetic philosophical framework. This is not ignorance but a different (and arguably more phenomenologically sophisticated) model of where the core of personhood resides.

WRAPPING, AMULETS, AND ANIMATION

After desiccation, the body was anointed with sacred resins and oils, stuffed with linen and fragrant substances, and wrapped in fine linen bandages in a specific sequence. As each layer was applied, amulets were placed at anatomically precise locations — the Udjat eye over the embalming incision, the Tyet at the throat, the Djed at the spine, the Heart Scarab over the heart. The wrapping itself was a Heka operation, each layer adding protection and reinforcing the identification of the body with the transfigured Osirian form.

THE IMIUT FETISH

A distinctive sacred object found in royal tombs — consisting of a stuffed, headless animal skin (possibly feline or bovine) attached to a pole and inserted into a jar — the Imiut (jmjwt, "that which is in its wrappings") was specifically sacred to Anubis and was present at every royal embalming. Its exact operative function is debated, but it appears to represent the boundary between the animal body and the transfigured form — the skin left behind when the essence departs — and to concentrate Anubis's presence and protective power in the embalming space.

THE EMBALMER AS ANUBIS

The chief embalmer — titled the Hery-seshta ("Master of Secrets") — wore a full Anubis mask during the most sacred phases of the preparation, particularly during the removal of the organs and the final wrapping. This was not theatrical costume but operative identification: by wearing the god's face, the practitioner literally became the vessel through which Anubis operated. The mask collapsed the distinction between human technician and divine patron, ensuring that the most critical operations were performed by the god himself, channelled through a trained human instrument.

// THE OPENING OF THE MOUTH — ANUBIS AS ACTIVATOR //

At the conclusion of the 70-day process, the completed mummy was brought to the tomb for the Opening of the Mouth ceremony — the final step that transformed the prepared Khat into a fully animated Sahu. While Anubis is not the primary officiant of this ritual (that role belongs to the Sem-Priest in the guise of Horus), his preparation of the body made the ceremony possible. The Opening of the Mouth animated what Anubis had prepared. Without Anubis's 70-day alchemy, there would be nothing for the wp-r to animate. The two rituals are complementary operations: Anubis creates the vessel; the Opening of the Mouth fills it.

IV. Psychopomp and the Hall of Judgment

Anubis's role as psychopomp — the divine conductor of souls between the living world and the afterlife — is one of his most ancient and continuously attested functions. In the Pyramid Texts, he leads the deceased king through the gates of the horizon; in the Middle Kingdom Coffin Texts, he guides any person of sufficient ritual preparation; in the New Kingdom Book of the Dead and the illustrated Papyri of Ani and Hunefer, he is the visible hand that escorts the soul into the Hall of Two Truths.

The iconic psychopomp image from the Papyrus of Hunefer (British Museum, c. 1275 BCE) shows Anubis with his left hand on the wrist of Hunefer — not grasping, not pulling, but accompanying with a gentle precision that communicates both authority and care. This gesture is the definitive image of the Anubis relationship: he knows exactly where the soul needs to go; he ensures it gets there; but the accompaniment is not coercive. He is the guide who cannot be refused but who does not need to use force.

The Weighing of the Heart — Anubis as Keeper of the Balance

Within the Hall of Two Truths, Anubis performs the most technically exacting operation of the entire judgment process: he places the deceased's heart in one pan of the balance scale and the feather of Ma'at in the other, then reads the indicator needle with absolute precision. Thoth records the result; Osiris presides as judge; Ammit waits. But the actual measurement is Anubis's.

The fitness of Anubis for this role is theologically elegant. The god who spent 70 days managing the most delicate physical transformation of mortal matter — weighing the precise amounts of natron, timing the removal of organs, judging the correct degree of desiccation — is exactly the being whose precision you want operating the cosmic scales at the moment of final accountability. His capacity for exact measurement is not a new skill acquired for the Hall; it is the same skill he has always exercised, applied at the highest possible stakes.

"Anubis, who is upon his mountain, who is in the place of embalming, the lord of the Sacred Land, weighed the heart of the justified one against the feather, and found it true." — After Book of the Dead Spell 125, standard Negative Confession context

Keeper of the Duat's Gates

Beyond the Hall itself, Anubis is described in the Netherworld Books as the master of the threshold — the one who holds the passwords to each of the 42 gates of the Duat, knows the secret names of their guardians, and can ensure a soul's passage through the complex topography of the afterlife. In the Book of Gates specifically, Anubis appears at critical thresholds as the gatekeeper whose permission is required for passage. His knowledge of the Duat is total — not merely its dangerous passages but its geometry, its guardians, its logic.

This comprehensive knowledge of death's landscape is inseparable from his other roles: a deity who invented mummification necessarily understands where the mummy's soul will travel; a psychopomp who escorts every soul to the Hall has traversed every path of the Duat countless times. Anubis's mastery of the threshold is not theoretical but experiential — he has walked every route, spoken every password, negotiated every gate, in service of the souls he has guided.

V. Divine Epithets and Titles

Egyptian divine epithets are not merely honorific titles — they are operative descriptions of specific functional aspects of the deity's power. Each epithet of Anubis encodes a precise theological statement about one dimension of his role in the cosmic system. The full roster of his attested epithets reveals the extraordinary breadth of the Anubis principle.

VI. Anubis and Wepwawet — The Two Openers of Ways

Wepwawet (Wp-wAwt, "Opener of Ways") is a deity so closely associated with Anubis that ancient and modern interpreters have repeatedly confused or conflated them. Both are depicted as black or grey canids — Wepwawet as a wolf or wolf-headed man, Anubis as a jackal or jackal-headed man — both preside over the transition of souls, and both appear together in funerary processions and at the heads of royal expeditions. Understanding their distinction is essential to understanding both.

WEPWAWET — THE WAY-OPENER

Wepwawet's name is his function: he opens the way. His role is specifically the dissolution of obstacles — the clearing of the path before the main party arrives. In funerary processions, the Wepwawet standard was carried at the front, preceding the coffin. In royal military expeditions, Wepwawet led the way before the king's army. He is the advance scout, the pathfinder, the being who goes ahead to clear, test, and mark the route. His origin is at Asyut (ancient Lycopolis — "City of the Wolf") in Upper Egypt.

ANUBIS — THE GUARDIAN AND GUIDE

Where Wepwawet opens the way, Anubis ensures the traveller completes it correctly. Anubis accompanies the soul, manages its transition at each threshold, performs the weighing at the Hall, and monitors the soul's integrity throughout the journey. His role is not to clear the path but to shepherd the person through a path already laid out — ensuring it is navigated with the precision the system demands. Together, they constitute a complete transition technology: Wepwawet prepares the road; Anubis guides the soul along it.

THE SED FESTIVAL CONNECTION

In the royal Sed Festival — the pharaoh's 30-year ritual renewal — it was specifically the Wepwawet standard that led the processional, opening the way for the king's passage between the two thrones of Upper and Lower Egypt. This positioning confirms Wepwawet's role as the herald and path-opener in royal ceremonial contexts, while Anubis presided over the more intimate, interior dimension of the king's spiritual renewal.

THE CANID CONFUSION

The persistent confusion between the two deities in popular sources often reflects the genuine ambiguity that existed even in ancient Egypt. Some texts show their functions bleeding into each other; some images could be identified as either deity depending on context. The ambiguity is not error but theological depth: both express aspects of the same fundamental principle of threshold management, differentiated by emphasis (way-opening vs. way-guiding). They are two resonances of the same divine frequency, not two entirely separate entities.

VII. Anubis in the Greek Magical Papyri — Hermanubis

The veneration of Anubis not only survived the Greek and Roman conquest of Egypt but flourished in new forms, acquiring a rich second life in the Greco-Roman religious and magical world. By the Ptolemaic and Roman periods, Anubis had become one of the most widely recognised Egyptian deities in the Mediterranean world — depicted on coins, worshipped in Isiac temples throughout the empire, and extensively invoked in the operative magical literature that became the Greek Magical Papyri (PGM).

Hermanubis — The Syncretic Synthesis

The syncretic fusion of Anubis with Hermes — forming Hermanubis (Ἑρμανοῦβις) — was one of the most theologically coherent syncretisms of the Hellenistic period. Both deities served as psychopomps — guides of souls between the living world and the realm of the dead. Both were associated with wisdom, with hidden knowledge, and with the communication between divine and human realms. Both were depicted with caduceus-adjacent symbols in syncretic imagery. The merger was not imposed but emerged naturally from the genuine resonance between two traditions' approaches to the same divine function.

Hermanubis was depicted as a youthful figure combining the jackal head of Anubis with the caduceus and winged sandals of Hermes — or as a fully human figure with simply the addition of a small jackal head as a crown element. His worship spread throughout the Roman Empire alongside the Isiac religious complex, with which Anubis had always been intimately associated. The Emperor Commodus (reigned 177–192 CE) reportedly participated in Isiac processional rites wearing an Anubis mask — evidence of how deeply the god had penetrated Roman religious practice.

PGM Invocations — Anubis as Operative Ally

In the PGM corpus (c. 2nd century BCE to 5th century CE), Anubis appears as a primary paredros — a divine magical assistant who can be ritually engaged for specific operative purposes. // A TYPICAL PGM INVOCATION OF ANUBIS — STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS //

PGM invocations of Anubis follow a consistent structural pattern: an opening sequence of voces magicae (barbarous names — strings of vowels and divine names in Egyptian, Greek, and Coptic), followed by a direct address to Anubis by his accumulated epithets ("You who dwell in the sacred land, you who guard the embalmed..."), then a statement of the practitioner's purpose and a specific request. The ritual often involves the burning of specific incense (kyphi and myrrh are most commonly specified — both with strong Egyptian funerary associations), the drawing of a seal or image of the canid deity, and sometimes the use of a dog's skull or bone as a material link.

The multilingual, multi-traditional character of these texts — moving between Greek, Demotic Egyptian, and Coptic within a single papyrus — is direct evidence that Anubis survived the cultural transitions not by being simplified into a foreign framework but by being genuinely recognized as carrying real operative power that transcended any single linguistic or cultural expression.

VIII. Alchemical and Esoteric Associations

Anubis as the Nigredo — The Black Work

In the alchemical framework — which draws directly on Egyptian funerary symbolism for its foundational stage-map — Anubis is the presiding intelligence of the Nigredo (the Black Work): the first and most essential stage of the Great Work, characterised by dissolution, putrefaction, and the breaking down of all established structures. The Nigredo is not an obstacle to transformation; it is transformation's necessary precondition. You cannot build the Philosopher's Stone on unrefined material. The existing form must first be reduced to its prima materia — its fundamental, undifferentiated substance — before the reconstruction into a higher form becomes possible.

Anubis's black colouration identifies him directly with this stage. His 70-day embalming process is itself an alchemical operation: the Nigredo (natron desiccation — decomposition arrested and redirected), followed by the preparation for Albedo (the purification and linen-wrapping phase), leading ultimately to the Rubedo of the Opening of the Mouth (the animation of the fully prepared vessel). The entire mummification sequence maps onto the alchemical color stages with a precision that is not coincidental — both the alchemists and the embalmers were working from the same understanding of how matter must be processed to achieve permanent transformation.

NIGREDO — THE DISSOLUTION

The death of the old form. In mummification: the removal of organs, the packing in natron, the surrender of the body's biological integrity to a controlled process of dissolution. In inner work: the collapse of the old identity structure, the disorientation of established reference points, the "dark night" before the new form becomes visible. Anubis presides as the witness who does not flinch from the dissolution process — who sees its necessity and its dignity.

THE INCORRUPTIBLE MATTER

The alchemical goal of creating matter that cannot be further degraded — incorruptible gold — directly mirrors the mummification goal of creating a body that cannot further decay. Both are attempts to stabilise a physical substrate at its highest possible frequency, permanently arresting the cycle of degeneration. Anubis's mastery of this process in the funerary domain is the original template from which the alchemical concept was derived.

Anubis in Esoteric Tradition — The Threshold Keeper

In the Western esoteric tradition that descended from Hermetic and Neoplatonic engagement with Egyptian theology, Anubis occupies a specific functional position: he is the principle of discernment at the threshold. Before the soul can enter any higher domain — the Hall of Judgment, the fields of Iaru, the solar realm — it must first pass through Anubis's assessment. He does not judge in the way Osiris judges; he assesses readiness, manages the transition, and ensures that what passes through the threshold is what it claims to be.

This threshold-discernment function maps onto the esoteric concept of the Guardian of the Threshold — the figure encountered at the boundary between ordinary consciousness and higher states of awareness, who tests the sincerity and preparation of the aspirant before permitting access to the next level of initiation. In Theosophical and Rosicrucian frameworks specifically, this Guardian role was often illustrated using imagery drawn directly from the Anubis tradition, even when the connection was not explicitly acknowledged.

// THE JACKAL AND THE NAVIGATOR — METAPHYSICAL READING //

From the perspective of this archive's operative framework, Anubis represents a specific quality of consciousness that is essential to any deep transformative work: the willingness to be fully present at the threshold of dissolution without retreating, combined with the technical competence to manage the dissolution process precisely rather than allowing it to become chaotic. He is the difference between controlled alchemical dissolution and uncontrolled decomposition — the intelligence that keeps the Nigredo on track, preventing it from collapsing into mere destruction while also preventing premature closure before the dissolution is complete.

The practitioner who invokes Anubis is asking for this quality: the capacity to witness dissolution without panic; the precision to know when to intervene and when to allow the process to continue; and the navigational knowledge to move through the transformed material to its proper destination. These are not metaphors for psychological processes alone — they describe the actual technical requirements of any genuine threshold-crossing work, whether in inner development, shamanic practice, or operative ritual.

IX. Working with Anubis — The Living Tradition

Anubis remains one of the most consistently approached deities in contemporary Kemetic practice — and one of the most misunderstood. His popular image as a "death god" in the morbid, Western Halloween sense profoundly misrepresents his actual character. He is not the bringer of death, not its personification, and not a dangerous or malevolent intelligence to be approached with fear. He is the world's most competent and caring transition manager — a being of extraordinary precision, genuine compassion, and absolute reliability at the moment of greatest vulnerability.

WHEN TO APPROACH ANUBIS

Anubis is appropriately invoked in any context involving: the death or dying of loved ones (to ensure safe passage and proper transition); grief work (he witnessed the first grief, attended the first mourning); transitions and life thresholds (career change, relationship endings, major identity shifts); shadow work and Nigredo-stage inner alchemy; navigation of the liminal zone in shamanic or dream work; and any situation requiring honest assessment of what has ended and what remains.

TRADITIONAL OFFERINGS

Historically attested offerings to Anubis include: black candles or oil lamps; myrrh incense (the pre-eminent funerary resin); natron or salt; fresh water; black obsidian or jet; images of jackals or dogs. The colour black in materials is appropriate — not as morbid aesthetic but as participation in Anubis's own transformative darkness. Dogs and jackals as household animals were understood as his earthly representatives; kindness to dogs was considered respectful to Anubis.

THE QUALITY OF HIS ATTENTION

Experienced practitioners consistently report Anubis's presence as characterised by an extremely precise, unhurried attentiveness — the sense of being seen clearly and completely without judgment, assessed with absolute accuracy rather than reassured with comfortable imprecision. He is not a deity of soft comfort but of honest accompaniment. His care is expressed through precision rather than sentiment. Approaching him with false presentation or incomplete honesty typically results in his attention going elsewhere; he has no interest in appearances.

DISCERNMENT NOTE

Contemporary popular occultism has attached Anubis to a range of aesthetics and frameworks — "dark god" energy, death magic, necromancy — that partially capture but substantially distort his character. The Egyptian Anubis was not dangerous, not unpredictable, and not associated with cursing, harm, or the raising of hostile dead. He was the most reliable, most precise, and most caring guardian of the transition process. Contact that feels threatening, unpredictable, or chaotic is unlikely to be genuine Anubis resonance.

Academic study: Salima Ikram, Divine Creatures: Animal Mummies in Ancient Egypt (2005) — contextualises Anubis within the broader canid sacred animal tradition. John Gee, A Guide to the Book of the Dead (2019) — for Anubis's Hall of Judgment role in the fullest canonical context.

For the PGM material: Hans Dieter Betz (ed.), The Greek Magical Papyri in Translation (University of Chicago Press, 1986/1992) — the standard scholarly edition. Index references to Anubis/Hermanubis invocations provide immediate access to the operative texts.
