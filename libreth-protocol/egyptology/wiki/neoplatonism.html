Overview

Neoplatonism (3rd–6th centuries C.E.) represents the final phase of ancient Greek philosophy, heavily influenced by the metaphysical traditions of Egypt. Founded by Plotinus (born in Lycopolis, Egypt), it emphasizes the "One" (the Absolute), from which all reality emanates in a series of levels (Intellect, Soul, and Matter).

Unlike earlier Platonic thought, Neoplatonism sought a total synthesis of spiritual and rational knowledge, often incorporating "Theurgy" (divine work) to achieve union with the One.

Egyptian Theoretical Roots

Neoplatonist philosophers explicitly drew on Egyptian theology to ground their metaphysical systems. Iamblichus (in On the Mysteries of the Egyptians) argued that the ancient Egyptian priesthood possessed the original, superior wisdom given to humanity by the gods.

The "One" as Atum

The Neoplatonic concept of the Absolute (the One) parallels the Egyptian Atum, the primordial monad who brought himself into being from the void of Nun.

Theurgy and Heka

Neoplatonic Theurgy—the use of ritual to align the human soul with divine forces—is the direct philosophical descendant of Heka (Egyptian magic).

The Ladder of Ascent

Neoplatonism describes a Ladder of Ascent—a path of spiritual return to the One. This involves Catharsis (purification of the lower soul) followed by Theoria (contemplation of the higher forms). For the Alexandrian Neoplatonists, this process was aided by Sacred Geometry, viewed as the visual language of the Divine Mind (Nous).

Proclus & the Demiurge

In the works of Proclus, the Demiurge is not a malevolent figure (as in Gnosticism) but the "Divine Intellect" that shapes the world according to the patterns of the One. This mirrors the Kemetic Ptah, who translates the silence of Atum into the structure of physical reality.
