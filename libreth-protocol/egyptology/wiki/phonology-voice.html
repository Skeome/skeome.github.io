I. The Ontology of Sound — The Silence of the Glyphs

Ancient Egyptian writing systems (Hieroglyphs, Hieratic, Demotic) were primarily consonantal. They recorded the skeletal structure of words but omitted the short vowels. Consequently, the "living voice" of Kemet went silent for nearly 1,500 years.

In this research framework, the recovery of phonology is viewed as frequency calibration. Without the correct vowels, a magical formula (Heka) is like a machine without a power source. The vowels are the "breath" that animates the "bones" of the consonants, allowing for the achievement of Ma'at Kheru (True of Voice).

III. The Vocalic Problem — Reconstruction through Coptic

The primary source for reconstructing Egyptian vowels is Coptic, the final stage of the language, which used the Greek alphabet and included vowels. By tracing sound shifts backward, phonologists can estimate the vocalic structure of earlier periods.

THE NAME OF ISIS

In the New Kingdom, the name of Aset likely sounded closer to *ʔŪsat. By the Coptic period, this had shifted to Ēse. This progression allows us to "tune" our pronunciation to the specific era we wish to resonate with.

EGYPTOLOGICAL PRONUNCIATION

Scholars use a conventional system (inserting "e" between consonants) for communication. While practical, this is not a reconstruction of the ancient voice and lacks the resonant charge of the original phonology.
