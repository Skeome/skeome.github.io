The Cradle of Civilization (c. 4300–3100 BCE)

The foundations of Egyptian cosmology were not invented overnight. They emerged over more than a millennium of pre-state cultural evolution known as the Predynastic Period. This era is divided into three main phases of the Naqada Culture, named after the type-site in Upper Egypt where the sequence was first identified.

NAQADA I (Amratian) [4300–3650 BCE]

Characterized by black-topped red ware pottery. The first depictions of boats appear, possibly foreshadowing the Solar Barque. Ritual burials begin to show status differentiation.

NAQADA II (Gerzean) [3650–3300 BCE]

Expansion northwards. First narrative art (Tomb 100 at Hierakonpolis) showing the "Master of Animals" motif. Imported lapis lazuli signals long-distance trade. The layout of the cosmos begins to solidify in iconography.

NAQADA III / DYNASTY 0 [3300–3100 BCE]

State Formation. Emergence of the Serekh (palace façade) containing the Horus name of the king. Key centers like Nekhen (Hierakonpolis), Abydos, and This vie for supremacy. The first true hieroglyphs appear on ivory tags in Tomb U-j at Abydos (c. 3250 BCE).

Hierakonpolis [Nekhen] & The Palettes

Hierakonpolis was the cult center of Horus the Falcon. It is the site of the "Main Deposit," a cache of ritual objects including the Narmer Palette and the Scorpion Macehead. These Cosmetic Palettes were not for daily use but were votive objects used to grind pigments for cult statues, depicting the ritual subduing of Isfet (chaos) by the ruler.
