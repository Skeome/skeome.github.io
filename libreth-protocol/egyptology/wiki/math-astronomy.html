Egyptian Mathematics

Egyptian mathematics was a practical, problem-solving system. A profound application of this science is found in the Wedjat Eye amulet, where the component parts of the eye correspond exactly to fractions of the heqat grain measure (1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/32, 1/64), encoding sacred geometry into a protective talisman.

The Rhind Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE)

The most extensive mathematical document, containing 84 problems. It includes the calculation of the area of a circle using a value for Pi (approximately 3.16), and the first systematic use of Unit Fractions—representing all fractions as the sum of reciprocals (e.g., 2/3 = 1/2 + 1/6).

The Moscow Papyrus (c. 1850 BCE)

Contains one of the most sophisticated achievements of ancient geometry: the formula for the volume of a truncated pyramid (frustum). This demonstrates a deep understanding of three-dimensional spatial awareness long before the Greeks.

Astronomy & The Decanal Clocks

Astronomy was deeply integrated into Egyptian religion and temple orientation. The Egyptians were the first to develop a 365-day solar calendar, divided into three seasons (Akhet, Peret, and Shemu).

Decans: The 36 Star Groups

The 36 Decans were specific constellations that rose consecutively every ten days. These were used to create Star Clocks on the inside of coffin lids, allowing the deceased to tell the time in the night sky. Each Decan was associated with a specific deity and possessed a unique vibrational frequency used in the timing of Heka rituals.

The Dendera Zodiac

The Dendera Zodiac, a bas-relief from the ceiling of the Temple of Hathor at Dendera, is one of the most famous astronomical artifacts from the Ptolemaic period. It represents a synthesis of traditional Egyptian decans and Babylonian/Greek zodiacal constellations, showing a sophisticated understanding of the night sky and the precession of the equinoxes.
