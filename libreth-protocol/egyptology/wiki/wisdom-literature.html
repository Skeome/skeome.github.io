The Genre of "Teaching"

Sebayt (literally "Teaching" or "Instruction") is one of the oldest and most significant genres of Egyptian literature. These texts are ethical manuals written by a father (usually a sage or vizier) to his son, providing guidance on how to live in accordance with Ma'at.

Unlike the ritual texts of the temples, Sebayt focuses on social behavior, self-control (the "Silent Man"), and the moral cultivation of the individual. They are the practical application of Egyptian metaphysics in daily life.

Key Texts

Instruction of Ptahhotep

Old Kingdom (Dynasty 5). The oldest complete book in the world. It emphasizes humility, listening, and the power of persuasive speech over brute force.

Instruction of Amenemope

New Kingdom (Dynasty 20). A masterpiece of personal piety. It contains 30 chapters on protecting the weak and respecting boundaries. It famously parallels Proverbs 22:17–24:22 in the Bible.

Instruction of Any

New Kingdom (Dynasty 18). Written for the common man rather than the elite bureaucracy, focusing on domestic life and piety toward the gods.

The Eloquent Peasant

A narrative tale about the power of Ma'at and rhetoric, where a humble peasant uses superior speech to win justice from the high steward.

The Instruction of Amenemhat I

A unique sub-genre of Sebayt is the Royal Instruction. The Instruction of Amenemhat I for his son Senusret I was written after the king's assassination. It is a cynical, high-stakes manual on the dangers of power, famously warning: "Trust no brother, know no friend, make for yourself no intimates."

Political Metaphysics

These texts reveal that Ma'at was not just a passive balance, but a dynamic and often brutal requirement of statecraft. The king had to impose order upon a recalcitrant world, often at great personal cost to his Ba. Wisdom was the armor required to survive the "dissonance of men."
